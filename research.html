Endogenous Bargaining Weights in Search and Match Labour Markets

▸Abstract

In frictional labour markets wages are determined through a bargaining process in which firms and workers share the surplus they jointly generate. A crucial element in this process is the bargaining weight, usually treated as exogenous. I develop an alternating-moves bargaining model where firms and workers continuously meet new potential partners during negotiations; outside options evolve in real time, leading to endogenous breakdown probabilities. Bargaining weights become functions of market tightness: as tightness rises, workers secure a larger share of the surplus.

Effects of State Aid on Firm Performance: Evidence from 22 EU Countries

with Andrea Brasili, Annamaria Tueske, Jochen Schanz

▸Abstract

Using large-award data merged with firm-level accounts (ORBIS), we estimate a staggered difference-in-differences design. State aid boosts investment—especially under regional-development programmes—but does not raise employment and initially lowers productivity. Heterogeneity matters: firms in Central and Eastern Europe, and those in sectoral schemes, show more favourable productivity responses.

The Ripple Effect: Children's Job Insecurity and Parents' Labour and Consumption Choices

with Elena Lazzaro

▸Abstract

Exploiting Italy’s 2012 “Fornero” reform—which cut firing costs only for firms > 15 employees—we show that parents of treated young adults reduced consumption by €2,453 per year, driven entirely by durables. Labour supply does not adjust, but the consumption drop is sharper for already-retired parents who cannot work more and hence reduce even non-durables.

Labour Demand and Firing Costs under Imperfect Competition: A Non-Monotonic Relationship

▸Abstract

In an oligopolistic setting, firing costs can raise hiring because they let firms credibly commit to higher output, deterring rivals. At low levels, costs hinder hiring; at high levels, they enable leadership and expansion. Strategic spillovers mean firms react to competitors’ constraints, not only their own—challenging the standard “firing costs = fewer jobs” view.
